In English, we say
how quickly things can change.
In poetry, we say
probably friends,
possibly lovers,
but definitely not nothing.

Those were the three thoughts in my mind
the first time your path crossed mine,
the first time I met your eyes
and felt time do that quiet stutter
it pretends it doesn’t know how to do.

Then I learned your favorite color.
Watched how easily you painted a smile
across my serious disposition.
I started practicing learning you,
thinking about you a little too much
in the margins of my spare time.

And the three thoughts rearranged themselves.
Probably lovers,
possibly friends,
but definitely not nothing.

I have a crush on you,
but not in the modern sense
where affection is archived
and measured in taps and lurks.
I mean in the way I want to record your laugh,
play it back to God,
and thank Him on everyone’s behalf.

I mean in the way that for you,
I would be put together.
I would deconstruct my worst habits,
because for you
I would want to be better.
And somehow,
in doing that,
I would become better for myself.

You inspire a new perspective.
You hold up a mirror and say
you should really be more perceptive.
You reflect the best parts of me,
but make room for the worst,
improvising as we go,
because love shouldn’t be rehearsed.

Those spaces might fill.
I might not be who you thought.
I might be someone better
than the idea you first bought.
And if you ever had a crush on me,
the real me,
I hope you’d grab a journal
and a fully loaded pen.

Write me a letter.
Say,
I know how this might go,
but I’m sick of being scared
of a feeling I really want to know.

Serendipity, they call it,
as I write my own letter too,
to say
I know how this might go,
but I have a huge crush on you.

Only one letter is sent.
The other stays folded in my chest.

Because I have a crush on you
in the running-through-the-airport sort of way.
The kind that risks embarrassment,
missed flights,
and everything unplanned.

That letter is hand delivered.
And I wouldn’t have it any other way.
